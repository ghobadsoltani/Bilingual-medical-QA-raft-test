Title: Type 2 Diabetes — Overview for Patients and Caregivers

Type 2 diabetes is a chronic condition affecting how the body uses glucose (sugar). In type 2 diabetes, cells become less responsive to insulin (insulin resistance), and the pancreas may not produce enough insulin to keep blood glucose in a healthy range. It is the most common form of diabetes in adults and is closely linked to genetics, age, excess weight, and physical inactivity.

Typical symptoms can include increased thirst, frequent urination, fatigue, blurred vision, slow-healing cuts, and unexplained weight changes. Some individuals are diagnosed during routine blood tests before symptoms appear. Diagnosis is usually based on fasting blood glucose, HbA1c (a measure of average glucose over ~3 months), or an oral glucose tolerance test interpreted by a clinician.

Management goals generally focus on keeping blood glucose, blood pressure, and cholesterol within targets agreed with a healthcare team, while reducing complications. Core strategies include balanced nutrition (often emphasizing whole grains, vegetables, lean proteins, and limited sugary drinks), regular physical activity, weight management when appropriate, and medication when needed.

Medications may include metformin as a common first-line option, plus other oral agents or injectable therapies including GLP-1 receptor agonists and insulin in more advanced cases. Self-monitoring of blood glucose may be recommended depending on treatment plan. Foot care, annual eye exams, and kidney function monitoring are important preventive steps.

Hypoglycemia (low blood sugar) can occur with certain medications, especially insulin or sulfonylureas. Warning signs may include shakiness, sweating, confusion, and irritability. Severe hypoglycemia is a medical emergency. Hyperglycemia (very high blood sugar) with vomiting, dehydration, or altered consciousness also requires urgent care.

People with diabetes should follow personalized plans from licensed providers. Educational materials support learning but do not replace diagnosis or treatment decisions.
