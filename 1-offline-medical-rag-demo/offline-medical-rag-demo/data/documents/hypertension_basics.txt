Title: Understanding High Blood Pressure (Hypertension)

High blood pressure, also called hypertension, is a common condition where the force of blood against artery walls stays too high over time. Blood pressure is recorded as two numbers: systolic (pressure when the heart beats) over diastolic (pressure when the heart rests). A reading of 120/80 mmHg or below is generally considered normal for adults. Readings consistently at or above 130/80 mmHg may indicate hypertension, though diagnosis should be confirmed by a healthcare provider using repeated measurements.

Common risk factors include age, family history, excess sodium intake, low physical activity, obesity, chronic stress, tobacco use, and heavy alcohol consumption. Many people have no obvious symptoms, which is why hypertension is sometimes called a "silent" condition. Untreated high blood pressure can increase the risk of heart disease, stroke, kidney disease, and vision problems.

Lifestyle approaches often recommended as part of a care plan include regular aerobic exercise (such as brisk walking), a diet rich in vegetables and fruits with limited added salt, maintaining a healthy weight, limiting alcohol, and avoiding tobacco. Home blood pressure monitoring can help track trends between clinic visits.

Medications may be prescribed when lifestyle changes alone are not enough. Common classes include ACE inhibitors, angiotensin receptor blockers (ARBs), calcium channel blockers, thiazide diuretics, and beta-blockers. Treatment is individualized; patients should not stop or change medications without medical guidance.

When to seek urgent care: sudden severe headache with vision changes, chest pain, shortness of breath, weakness on one side of the body, or blood pressure readings far above a person's usual range may require emergency evaluation.
